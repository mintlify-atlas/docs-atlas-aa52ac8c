## Tips for VibeVoice-TTS

<Tip>
  VibeVoice-TTS code is currently disabled. The tips below apply to running model weights directly through the Hugging Face transformers API if you are doing custom research work.
</Tip>

### Chinese speech stability

Users may encounter occasional instability when synthesizing Chinese speech. The following practices help:

- Use **English punctuation** even for Chinese text — prefer commas (`,`) and periods (`.`) only
- Use the **Large model variant** (`VibeVoice-Large`), which is considerably more stable for Chinese
- If the generated voice speaks too fast, **chunk your text** into multiple speaker turns with the same speaker label

### Spontaneous background music

The model can spontaneously generate background music or ambient sounds. This is an intentional emergent behavior from training on non-denoised data. To minimize unwanted BGM:

- Use a **clean voice prompt** (no background music in the reference audio)
- Avoid opening phrases like "Welcome to", "Hello", or "However" — these tend to trigger BGM
- Avoid the "Alice" speaker voice, which has a higher BGM rate
- The **Large model** is more stable and has lower probability of unexpected background music

### Text that may cause issues

- **Code and formulas** — pre-process to remove or normalize before synthesis
- **Uncommon symbols** — normalize to plain text equivalents
- **Chinese quotation marks** — may occasionally cause pronunciation issues
- **Very short inputs** (3 words or fewer) — model stability may degrade

## FAQ

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Is VibeVoice-TTS a pretrained model or fine-tuned?">
    It is a **pretrained model** without any post-training or benchmark-specific optimizations. This makes it very versatile — it handles a wide range of inputs and styles — but also means outputs can sometimes be unpredictable or include emergent behaviors not seen in more constrained models.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Why does the model sometimes generate background music or sounds?">
    The training data was intentionally **not denoised**, so the model learned to generate contextually appropriate ambient sounds and music. This is a feature, not a bug — the model is content-aware, meaning these sounds are triggered by the input text and voice prompt characteristics.

    The Large model variant is more stable and has a lower probability of generating unexpected background music.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Does the model perform text normalization?">
    **No.** The philosophy is that a large language model should handle complex inputs directly. However, due to training data characteristics, corner cases exist — particularly with code, mathematical formulas, uncommon symbols, and Chinese special characters.

    Pre-process input text to remove or normalize problematic content before synthesis.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Can the model sing?">
    Yes, this is an **emergent capability**. The training data contains no music, so singing ability emerged naturally from language model generalization. It may sound off-key on well-known songs. The Large model variant is more likely to exhibit singing than the 1.5B model.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Why are there Chinese pronunciation errors?">
    The volume of Chinese training data is significantly smaller than English. Additionally, certain special characters (e.g., Chinese quotation marks `「」` and `『』`) may occasionally cause pronunciation issues. Using the Large model and English punctuation improves stability.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="How stable is cross-lingual transfer?">
    The model exhibits **strong but unstable** cross-lingual transfer, including preservation of accents. This is an emergent capability not specifically optimized. If you don't get a satisfactory result on the first attempt, repeated sampling may produce better outputs.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="How can I control emotion in generated speech?">
    Community member [PsiPi](https://huggingface.co/PsiPi) discovered an interesting method for emotion control — see [HuggingFace discussion #12](https://huggingface.co/microsoft/VibeVoice-1.5B/discussions/12) for details.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## Limitations summary

| Limitation | Details |
|------------|---------|
| Languages | English and Chinese primary; other languages unpredictable |
| Overlapping speech | Model does not generate overlapping speech segments |
| Code/formulas | Not supported; pre-process input |
| Non-speech audio | Cannot generate background noise, music (unless emergent), or sound effects on demand |
| Bias | Inherits biases from Qwen2.5-1.5B base model |
| Commercial use | Not recommended without further testing |
